Sustainable Materials for
Eco-Friendly Interiors

The Neo Studio

Sustainability in interior design has moved well beyond bamboo floors and recycled glass. Today, it's a comprehensive approach to material selection, sourcing, longevity, and end-of-life — and it produces interiors that are not only responsible but genuinely beautiful.

Why Material Choice Matters

The materials in your home have a lifecycle that begins long before they arrive on site and continues long after you move out. Choosing materials with low embodied carbon, responsible sourcing, and long lifespans is the single most impactful sustainability decision in any interior project.

Embodied carbon — the carbon emitted during the extraction, manufacturing, and transport of materials — accounts for a significant portion of a building's total carbon footprint. Choosing wisely here matters.

Key Principle

The most sustainable material is often the one already in your home. Before specifying new materials, consider what can be retained, restored, or repurposed from the existing space.

Engineered Wood Over Solid Wood

Engineered wood uses a fraction of the timber of solid wood while delivering comparable performance and aesthetics. High-quality engineered wood with low-VOC adhesives is our default recommendation for flooring, cabinetry, and furniture in sustainable projects.

Look for products certified by the Forest Stewardship Council (FSC) — this ensures the wood comes from responsibly managed forests. Avoid products with formaldehyde-based adhesives, which off-gas for years after installation.

The most sustainable interior is one that doesn't need to be redone in five years. Durability is the most practical form of sustainability.

Natural Fibre Textiles

Organic cotton, linen, jute, and wool are biodegradable, durable, and beautiful. They outperform synthetic fabrics on every sustainability metric and age with a character that synthetics can't replicate.

  • Linen: Exceptionally durable, gets softer with washing, low water footprint
  • Organic cotton: Grown without pesticides, biodegradable, widely available
  • Jute: Rapidly renewable, biodegradable, excellent for rugs and baskets
  • Wool: Natural insulator, fire-resistant, extremely durable for upholstery

Low-VOC Paints and Finishes

Volatile organic compounds (VOCs) in conventional paints off-gas for months after application, affecting indoor air quality and contributing to health issues. Low-VOC and zero-VOC paints are now available in the full range of colours and finishes — there's no aesthetic compromise involved.

We specify low-VOC paints as standard on all Neo Studio projects. The cost difference is minimal; the health benefit is significant, especially in bedrooms and children's rooms.

Reclaimed and Recycled Materials

Reclaimed wood, recycled metal, and upcycled stone add character and history to a space that new materials simply can't replicate:

  • Reclaimed teak: Aged character, exceptional durability, zero new deforestation
  • Antique brass hardware: Sourced from demolition projects, unique patina
  • Recycled glass tiles: Made from post-consumer glass, beautiful translucency
  • Salvaged stone: Quarried stone from demolished buildings, unique character

Durability is Sustainability

The most sustainable interior is one that doesn't need to be redone in five years. Choosing quality materials, professional installation, and timeless design over cheap, trend-driven choices is the most practical form of sustainable design.

A kitchen designed and built to last 20 years has a fraction of the environmental impact of one that needs replacing in 7. This is why we never compromise on material quality — it's not just about aesthetics, it's about responsibility.

Indoor Air Quality

Sustainable design isn't just about the planet — it's about the people living in the space. Indoor air quality is directly affected by material choices:

  • Specify low-VOC paints, adhesives, and sealants throughout
  • Choose solid wood or low-formaldehyde engineered wood for cabinetry
  • Use natural fibre rugs and textiles rather than synthetic alternatives
  • Incorporate plants — they genuinely improve air quality and wellbeing
